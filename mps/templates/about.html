Overview

    • Microphysiology Systems Database

      The Microphysiology Systems Database (MPS-Db) is a centralized resource, key to managing the design and application of Microphysiology Systems (MPS) experimental models. It is a platform to analyze data and interpret MPS model results in the context of human physiology, and design computational models. The MPS-Db is an open source, internet accessible website that aggregates preclinical and clinical data from a variety of open source and proprietary databases as along with experimental data from MPS models. Through a simple icon driven interface the MPS-Db enables the design and implementation of multifactor and multichip studies, the capture and standardization of MPS experimental data and metadata (description of the experimental design and conditions), and provides tools to analyze the data, and statistically assess the performance of the MPS models (e.g., reproducibility and power analysis). Data from any MPS organ model constructed on any type of platform from microplates to sophisticated, microfluidic devices evaluating any biological phenotype (e.g., secreted factors, cell morphology, cell viability, cellular and metabolic functions) measured in any assay (e.g., biosensors, ELISA, high content imaging, mass spec analysis) can be accommodated in the MPS-Db ( Gough et al. 2016).

      The MPS-Db website is managed by the University of Pittsburgh Drug Discovery Institute). Additional information about the MPS Database, included detailed instructions for using database features and contributing data to the database, can be found on the Help page. Questions and comments should be directed to MPSHELP@pitt.edu.

      The MPS-Db is funded by the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences Tissue Chips Program, the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences (NCATS) Tissue Chip Testing Centers Program, and in part by the Vanderbilt-Pittsburgh Resource for Organotypic Models for Predictive Toxicology.

Key Features

Contributing Data

    • Data Sources

      Experimentally derived assay data and associate metadata are loaded directly into the MPS Database by researchers performing the study. Additional data needed for computation modeling, data analysis, or for designing a study are curated into the MPS Database from other sources. Data are integrated together by the compound(s) used in experimental studies.

    • Data Access

      The MPS Database incorporates a multifaceted permission structure, allowing access to data to be granted in a multitude of configurations to match the needs of each individual user.

      User's Role Access to Data and Features
      All Registered MPS Database Users Can view all reference data and associated features and publicly available experimentally derived assay data.
      Data Providers Can add, edit, and share study data.
      Collaborators, Stakeholders, Funders Can review specific datasets prior to public release.
    • Sharing of Experimentally Derived Assay Data

      Researchers performing MPS tissue chips studies and contributing data to the MPS Database determine who can view their data. To meet the needs of the database user community, and the requirements of their funding agencies, the MPS Database offers a wide range of options for when and with whom data are shared.

      Option 1) The data provider can make their data publicly available at any time.
      Option 2) The data provider can share their data with a select group of colleagues.
      Option 3) The data provider signs-off a study to start a process of publicly releasing data after a year embargo.



      Data are grouped into studies and shared on a study-by-study basis. Typically, assay results are grouped into studies to facilitate comparison of treated to untreated tissue chips.

Frequently Asked Questions

    • Registration is easy and free. Just go to the MPS login page and click on the option to register, or just click here.
    • The MPS Database Help page provides detailed information and step-by-step examples on how to use the features of the MPS-Db. Click on the Help tab on the home page or click here to visit the page.
    • The database offers multiple ways of looking at compound data. Some users like to review the compound list, and sort, for example, by LogP. Then, use the global search to find additional information about promising compounds (such as, drug trials associated with a specific compound).
    • The MPS-Db is an open source, internet accessible website. Many of the features in the database are unrestricted and available to all database users.
    • To add assay data to the database, you must be assigned to a Data Group and granted the permission needed to add studies to and edit studies associated with a Data Group. These permissions must be granted by an MPS Database Administrator. Contact an administrator by sending an email to MPSHELP@pitt.edu with "Getting Setup to Contribute Data to the MPS Database" in the subject line. Detailed information on how to review and analyze data in the MPS Database is available on the database's Help page.
    • Researcher's who provide data are not required by the MPS Database Center to share their data. Studies that have been shared publicly will show on the Study List Page of the database. Studies that you have been granted permission to view can also be accessed through the inter-study analysis feature, and when reviewing the Target/Analytes and Method/Kits lists (available through the Studies->Study Components menu). Review the Help page to learn about these features.
    • To access any data that are not publicly available, you must be a registered user of the MPS Database. Once you are registered, an MPS Database Administrator will grant you the appropriate permission to acknowledge that you have reviewed the relevant study data. After permission has been granted, the study (or studies) that are waiting for your review will show on the Study List Page of the database. Look for studies with a check mark, but without an eye or a pencil icon in the Review column.
